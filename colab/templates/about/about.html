    About CoLab

    CoLab is a platform designed for open and massively collaborative science.
    Ethos
    What are you trying to do here?

    We want to capture the spirit of the scientific conference and put it on the web. Brainstorming with colleagues from around the world shouldn't occur only within a narrow three-day window once a year. It should be a part of everyday science. We want to make it as easy to collaborate with someone across oceans as easy as collaborating with someone across the hall.

    We call these spontaneous, transient interactions "microcollaborations." Less formal than an official collaboration, they focus on one single issue and often occur between researchers in widely-separated disciplines. For instance, a neuroscientist analyzing data from spiking neurons might ask a quick signal processing question of an electrical engineer.

    What inspired the site?

    When we were little kids, we imagined big rooms full of scientists, tossing ideas back and forth and all working together to solve interesting problems. We were disappointed to later discover that many groups work in isolated offices, often spending months redoubling the efforts of others or struggling just an idea away from a breakthrough but without the right expertise in the room to make it happen. We still dream of a place where scientists from all disciplines gather to help each other out and solve problems. We hope that place is CoLab.

    But what's wrong with things the way they are now?

    We think that science should be conducted in the open. The current presentation of scientific work resembles an iceberg. Ten percent of the efforts (in the form of highly polished papers) are visible and the other 90 percent (in the form of conversations, null results, data, source code, and more) are hidden from view. That other 90 percent can be very interesting and open science is about exposing it.

    Why should I do science on the web?

    Easy Project Management

    • Keep track of all the projects you're working on or interested in
    • Access your projects anywhere and anytime
    • Maintain a public record who contributed to an idea and how

    Share Ideas with Collaborators

    • Work together online with scientists from around the world and from all disciplines
    • Bring new collaborators up to speed quickly
    • Allow other researchers who share your interests to find your work and contribute
    • Collaboratively and publicly draft papers to summarize results

    Share Ideas with the Public

    • Enable members of the public to follow your work and results
    • Empower young scientists to see the process of science as it unfolds

    An Adaptive Site

    If there is one thing we want our users to understand, it is that CoLab will constantly be adapting to the needs of its users - scientists. This is not a finished version of the site and there never will be one. We have many new features currently in development and are constantly thinking about how to improve the site.

    If you are interested in what we are cooking up, check out what we're up under the CoLab Development Discipline, but even more importantly, let us know what you think! Found a bug? Don't like the UI? Itching for some new feature? Tell us! You can always participate in the CoLab Development Issues or use the feedback button on the left (on any page). You can also submit feedback directly in our Feedback system. We want to make the web a better place for scientists and we can't do it without your suggestions.

    Best of all, we don't have any hidden interests. We are not a publisher, a journal, or a government agency - just passionate scientists who want to help the community use the web to do science better.

    The Lab Rats
    Casey Stark @caseywstark - Founder and Architect

    Part web developer and part computational physicist, Casey would love to see science and the web learn to play nice. After completing several small web design projects last spring (2009), Casey decided to look into the intersection of the web and science. Frustrated with the lack of equation support and discussion tools out there for scientists, he began to tinkering with several ideas last summer and after months of coding, ended up with what you see here today. He implemented the entire site himself, so if something breaks, please yell at him. Casey is currently a graduating senior at the University of Southern California and will continue graduate studies in an as-yet-undetermined physics department in the fall.

    DJ Strouse @djstrouse- Co-Founder

    Easily excited but rarely satisfied, DJ entertained ideas of becoming a filmmaker, sherpa, and professional chef before selling his soul to science. His research interests are in the nature of information, computation, and intelligence and, in particular, their representation in the human brain. His scientific mecca is to understand the brain well enough to interface with and supplement neural computation. When he's not thinking about computation or hacking brains, he's thinking about hacking science and education. Currently, he's a junior majoring in physics and mathematics at the University of Southern California.
